What is Spinal Tumor?

Spinal Tumor
A spinal tumor is an abnormal growth that develops within or around the spinal cord or vertebrae. These tumors can be benign (non-cancerous) or malignant (cancerous) and may originate in the spine (primary tumors) or spread from other parts of the body (secondary tumors). Spinal tumors can cause pain, neurological deficits, and structural damage, potentially leading to serious complications if not diagnosed and treated early.

Causes
The exact cause of primary spinal tumors is often unknown, but genetic mutations and inherited conditions like neurofibromatosis or von Hippel-Lindau disease can increase the risk. Secondary spinal tumors are usually caused by cancers such as lung, breast, or prostate cancer metastasizing to the spine. Risk factors for spinal tumors include a family history of cancer, exposure to radiation, and a compromised immune system.

Symptoms
Symptoms of a spinal tumor vary based on the tumor’s location and size and may include persistent back or neck pain, muscle weakness, numbness, or tingling in the limbs. Other symptoms include difficulty walking, loss of sensation, or problems with bladder or bowel control. Severe cases may lead to paralysis or significant loss of mobility.

Treatment
Treatment options for spinal tumors include observation for slow-growing benign tumors, radiation therapy, chemotherapy, and surgical removal for tumors causing neurological symptoms or structural instability. Pain management, physical therapy, and rehabilitation are also crucial components of the treatment plan to maintain functionality and quality of life.
